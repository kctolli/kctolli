#+RESULTS:
(this is too loud)

  The =morphify= function is essentially a /mapping/ function, which means it
  takes a passed-in function (=fun=) and a passed-in list (=lst=), and /maps fun
  over lst/, i.e., the results of calling the function =fun= on each element of
  the list =lst= are collected into a new list, which is returned.

  This shows one way (iteratively) a /map/ function could be implemented.
  Another way is /recursively/, as in:

#+BEGIN_SRC emacs-lisp :results silent
  (defun morphifyr (fun lst)
    (if (null lst)
        nil
      (append (list (funcall fun (first lst))) (morphifyr fun (rest lst)))))
#+END_SRC

  Explain how this version of =morphify= works.
#+end_note
#+begin_info
  The =morphify= function allows one to make modifications to a list, and copy it to a new list.
  The second version of =morphify= calls itself recursively in order to loop through the list.
#+end_info

#+begin_note
  Why is the sum of the reciprocals of the powers of two scaled by which power
  equal to two?
#+end_note
*** Rephrase
Why is \sum(1/2^n) == 2?
*** Approach
I will calculate the sum of \sum(1/2^n). Then I will make a table with many of 2's powers and the sum up to that point.
*** Solution
When one takes the limit as n \rightarrow \infin, the sum, \sum(1/2^n) approaches 2.
This is because of the fact that the sum of the reciprocals of the powers of any number will be equal to
that number over the previous number.
i.e. - \sum(1/2^n)== 2/1
\sum(1/3^n)== 3/2
\sum(1/4^n)== 4/3
\sum(1/5^n)== 5/4
\sum(1/6^n)== 6/5
